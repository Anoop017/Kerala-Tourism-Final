About Kerala

Kerala, situated at the southwestern tip of the Indian subcontinent, is a land of rich cultural heritage and historical significance. Renowned for its long-standing traditions in art, culture, and foreign trade, Kerala is also the Indian state with the highest literacy rate. It has made remarkable progress in education, healthcare, gender equality, social justice, and law and order. The state also boasts the lowest infant mortality rate in the country.

Lying between the Arabian Sea in the west and the Western Ghats (Sahyadris) in the east, Kerala spans an area of 38,863 square kilometers. It is one of the five states forming the linguistic and cultural region of South India, bordered by Tamil Nadu and Karnataka. Additionally, Mahe, a part of the Union Territory of Puducherry, is geographically located within Kerala. The Lakshadweep Islands in the Arabian Sea, though part of a Union Territory, share linguistic and cultural ties with Kerala.

Known as "God's Own Country", Kerala is celebrated for its palm-lined beaches, tranquil backwaters, and breathtaking landscapes. The state is blessed with lush tea gardens, dense forests, serene backwaters, and crisscrossing rivers. The Western Ghats, with their verdant hills and diverse wildlife, form Kerala’s eastern natural border, while the Arabian Sea stretches along its western coastline.

Kerala’s history is deeply rooted in ancient traditions, making it a hub for maritime trade for centuries. It is renowned for its traditional art forms like Kathakali and Mohiniyattam, its classical music, and the age-old healing system of Ayurveda. With a literacy rate exceeding 96%, Kerala stands as the most literate state in India and serves as a model for socio-economic development.

Kerala's tropical climate, monsoon rains, and diverse geography make it a year-round destination for travelers and nature enthusiasts. Its famous attractions include the backwaters of Alappuzha, the golden beaches of Kovalam, the hill stations of Munnar, and the wildlife sanctuaries of Thekkady. Offering a perfect blend of natural beauty, culture, and history, Kerala continues to captivate tourists from around the world.

Explore Rich Culture & Traditions on your Visit

Kerala is rich in cultural heritage and traditions that have been passed down through generations. From its delicious cuisine to its traditional attire and unique architecture, Kerala offers a glimpse into the soul of Indian culture.

Food of Kerala

The food of Kerala consists of a wide variety of vegetarian and non-vegetarian food. It includes fish, meat, poultry, and rice preparations, along with many spices. Kerala Sadya is a banquet meal prepared during functions and festivals. Kerala food uses coconut oil and milk in many preparations, such as idli, dosa, and curry.

Clothing and Traditional Dresses of Kerala

Women of Kerala wear sarees and blouses, especially set sarees called Kasavu during festivals. Men wear 'mundu', a long piece of garment wrapped around their waist. Younger generations generally prefer western clothing, but mundus are still common for casual occasions and sarees are worn for formal ones.

Folk Dance and Music

The folk dance of Kerala consists of the Kaikotti Kali dance, Kuthiyottam dance, Tiruvathira Dance, Thirayattam dance, Kakkarissi Kali, etc. Kanniyar Kali (also known as Desathukali) is a dance done in front of Goddess Bhagvaty. It is one of the oldest folk dances. It is a fast-moving dance to the rhythmic devotional folk tune. Kaikotti Kali folk dance (also known as Thiruvathirakali) is performed by women of Kerala by dancing in symmetric circles. It is often performed during festive seasons like Thiruvathira and Onam.

Festivals of Kerala

Onam is a 10-day harvest festival celebrated in Kerala in the month of August - September. The festival is celebrated with grandeur. There are fairs and contests for people to indulge in. The floors are decorated with flower designs; there are dances for celebration and a snake boat race (Aranmula Boat Race) contest called Vallamkali is also carried out. The tenth or the last day of Onam is said to be most important and is one of the most popular manifestations of the Culture of Kerala.

Rituals of Kerala

Most of the rituals are temple rituals. Poothanu Mathirayum is a ritual for Goddess Kali in which dancers dress up as Kaali Ma and dance at all shrines in the village to remove and destroy the evil demon Daarikan. Patayani is a week-long dance ritual for Goddess Kali. It is carried out on the banks of river Pamba. The dancers tell the story of how evil is destroyed by taking up various character roles. Midiyettu and Koothu are other such similar rituals.
